Mobile Virtual reality based Corneal Topography

Developed an economical mobile virtual reality based corneal topographer a non-invasive medical imaging technique for mapping the surface curvature of the cornea, the outer structure of the eye. Mobile VR us used to project patterns on the cornea and later a pipeline to capture and the process the images was developed.

Supervisor: Asst.Prof. Rusha Patra , IIIT Guwahati

Collaboration with Srujana Innovation Center

OVERVIEW

In eye diagnostics corneal topography is one of the important tests which includes measuring the corneal shape, which plays an important role in human vision. The diagnostic test is used in keratoconus detection, personalized and effective contact lens fitting, both in pre and post-procedures associated with refractive surgery and even in corneal transplants. This project presents an easy to use , economical and portable approach to perform corneal topographies without much of heavy equipments. The emitting patterns for the corneal topography have been bulky with hard designed conical discs which  is based on the Placido’s disk, the present project delves into a way where emitting patterns are placed in front of the patient’s eye in an immersive virtual reality scene simulated on a smart-phone. The entire test can now take place with different emitting patterns in a closed miniaturized dark room called the VR Headset. Observing the deformations or changes in the projected pattern one can identify, refractive conditions related to eye care like astigmatism, keratocony and estimate the patient’s corneal topography. In this proposal we use an android VR-enabled smart phone to emit the patterns which are simulated by using graphic shaders and played in a Virtual Reality scene, which are then captured by the eye tracking cameras in a VR headset. A matlab image processing pipeline was developed to enhance the captured images, remove noise, segment out the ring patterns, associate the emitted graphic patterns with the captured one to sample the signal, and finally estimate the corneal surface.

The estimated shape is then decomposed using Zernike Polynomials in components with specific optical meanings. To Build a Topographer it is necessary to have a pattern emitter which help in capturing the deformations on the cornea i.e. the patterns may range from concentric circles to even checkerboard patterns, a device to capture the surface of the cornea with the reflected pattern and a device with good processing hardware to run the corneal reconstruction algorithms and do the required processing on the images captured. These are indeed simple requirements to meet, and with the evolving android and smart-phone market with great hardware like Octa-core and multi core processors and also web cameras which are easily portable and compact, makes it easier to use a Virtual reality based Corneal topographer serving all the requirements needed to reconstruct the corneal surface and diagnose any eye problems associated with the corneal structure. In order to get the device running, we had the following blocks to be built.

VR Headset: A custom google cardboard inspired Virtual reality headset was built to accommodate extra optics and camera to capture the reflecting patterns from the cornea exactly perpendicular for easily implementing a 3D Reconstruction  of the cornea . The basic setup resembles a classic Head Mounted Display with converging lenses to focus the view on the display. The difference lies in cameras at the outer boundary of the body case , beam splitters and a circular LED-light array along the adjustable lens holders to illuminate the eye . Reflected  light passes through the converging lenses and is reflected towards the cameras via two tilted beam splitters . Display light passes unhindered towards lenses and eye. Body Case The body case encapsulates all internal components . A central barrier with a gap for the nose divides the display into two disjoint symmetric parts, one for each eye. The case is firmly closed and tightened with foamed material to avoid exterior stray light and covered with comfortable tissue, except at the nose tip to enable normal breathing. The dimensions of the body were determined by fitting it to several eye tests. For display We can integrate a smart phone into the virtual reality headset. Our prototype offered horizontal field of view of 86per eye. Eye Tracking Cameras For binocular eye tracking, integrated two low-cost cameras focusing at the distance of the user’s eyes and having a fixed diagonal field of view of 56.The cameras have fixed positions in the Head Mounted Display and record at a 1280 x 960 optical resolution in gray scale and frame rate of 30fps i.e. 640x480 with a delay of 13 ms due to the internal image processor. This sampling rate suffices to track fixations and smooth pursuit eye movements

Android VR App: In order to reflect the patterns of the placido rings we have opted to use virtual patterns in a VR scene so as to have the freedom and easiness to change the patterns without any material requirements and also change the contrast and lighting in the dark VR headset based on the requirements. A Unity Based Virtual Reality android application was developed. We used google Cardboard SDK for unity and depend on the Unity shaders to develop the virtual patterns. In order to simulate the pattern, there had to be study done based on the mathematics of the pattern simulation, use of OpenGL and also other unity features to make an android version of the application. Image Processing: To automatically estimate the corneal surface. We need to associate each border in the captured image with a ring from the emitted pattern. First, we find the eye centroid and crop the region around it. After that, we stretch the image contrast and correct the image lightness using morphological algorithms. Next, we extract the patterns using differences of Gaussians. Finally, the resulting image is enhanced to simplify border detection. Finally the captured image from the ring is sampled along meridians, illustrated on the top-left. The reconstruction is performed for each meridian, in point-pair steps (top-right). Iteratively, the algorithm searches for the next position, based on the approach restrictions: the two points are connected by a circle arc, preserving the first point normal and respecting the law of reflection in the second point (bottom-right). Finally, the process continues calculating the next point along the meridian. Refer to the images below for step by step Processing
