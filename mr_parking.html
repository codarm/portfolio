OVERVIEW

Utilizing Urban Spaces with responsibility becomes very important to maintain the discipline and rhythm of the daily activities in a city. Parking spaces being urban dominant, it becomes important to enforce rules around their utility. Fair parking enforcement becomes critical for good transportation policy, helping in reducing congestion and dangerous conditions, also increasing turnover for the policymakers. As a result, proper management and optimization of parking enforcement officers’ time help in increasing access and making conditions safer for pedestrians and bicyclists.

The Solution for this wicked Problem of Parking & its Enforcement can be categorized  as follows:

Performance Management: Measuring parking enforcement activity is important with many metrics & outcomes, and cities owe it to their constituencies to analyze the data. Issuing lots of citations doesn’t necessarily help improve the city experience. Hence the enforcement agencies should review the quality of citation issuance by every PEO.

New Enforcement Zones: Enforcement boundaries aren’t modified for years, despite changing demand and use. Cities are often prone to dynamic contexts due to ever-evolving geospatial changes and are important to use this dynamic context data to improve the size and shape of the zones, resulting in reducing predatory enforcement, or the potential of PEO’s to focus too much on a neighborhood or even vehicles in some cases. Using algorithmic interventions to revise enforcement maps, nudging boundaries, and planning could help the PEO’s in achieving equity and increasing productivity by capturing dynamic trends.

 

In this Section of the Project, I have tried to tackle and answer the Following questions or scenarios.

  1. Are citations accurate? Erroneous and poorly written citations cause motorists unnecessary pain and waste time and resources.
  2. Are there too many lengthy gaps between citations? We can study latent time—or periods of inactivity—as well as patrol periods, to improve productivity.
  3. Experiential Journey with less Cognitive load to issue citations in dynamic urban contexts, and also have collaborative issuance within the officers.

 

Urban Planning, enforcement, and design require iterative and collaborative Toolkits for the stakeholders to make decisions. Parking Enforcement is one such activity that involves significant guesswork and the current tools deployed in the field offer virtual environments and single user experience. The current tools may put the officers in a context to make assumptions, and thereby lead to inequitable, predatory enforcement (or the disproportionate issuance of citations in underserved neighborhoods or socio-economically deprived communities) and enforcement canvassing that may be inconsistent with the need to mitigate congestion, increase turnover, and promote public safety. The Conventional solutions have involved the generation of static HTML maps with a probability of infractions, congestion mitigation, or other regulatory enforcement on a block-by-block basis and optimization routes, but unfortunately, these tools do not account or incorporate PEO’s real-time location, real-time behavior/contexts such as blocks that may be already enforced (or real-time changes in violation probabilities due to public complaints or feedback from various technologies like sensors, CCTV, and parking meters), leaving the PEO to make inferences open ended.

 

Research to identify an optimum strategy for enforcement routing has involved studying numerous optimization strategies as mentioned in other sections in my portfolio. Spatio-temporal probabilistic modeling strategies have been incorporated using real-time sensor data to propose solutions to the “Travelling Officer” problem, which aims to obtain maximum citations in a minimum amount of time. There has also been some research introducing game-theory modeling to identify potential interactions between an enforcement officer's decision to patrol an area and corresponding decisions by a driver to pay for parking. While most of these solutions focus on deriving an effective model, there is a noticeable void in addressing how these models may improve an enforcement officer's day-to-day work. The present invention considers this to be a foundational direction upon which to build, addressing an evident gap in enforcement routing applications—the ability to view the context for a certain route and complete that route in the shortest time possible with the most citation captures rendered by the assistance of visual aids along the route. Navigation can involve four primary tasks: initial orienting, maneuvering, orientation maintenance, and target recognition. Varied strategies for effective navigation have been explored over time. Successful navigation can generally involve beacons and landmarks (to identify location through place markers and configurations), route knowledge (to identify locations through corresponding sequence), graph knowledge (connections of places (nodes) through intersecting paths), survey knowledge (to estimate location through metric measurement), and prokinetic knowledge (to learn about spatiality through motor movement and muscle contractions along with visual knowledge). Key factors that can influence the design of navigation systems, however, are localization and orientation sources and technologies, the varied number and types of output devices, and the level of abstraction created to bridge the gap between the real and virtual worlds. The need for establishing a context can be underscored within a transitional interface—an interaction technique to navigate, change content within a context, or transition between contexts. In addition, context can be defined as an environment where a user can interact and collaborate, and include space (e.g. augmented reality, virtual reality, reality), scale (e.g. macro, micro, nano), representation (e.g. photorealistic, symbolic), viewpoints (e.g. focus, secondary), and embodiment (e.g. proximal, distal).
