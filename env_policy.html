Equitable Smart Grid - MIT Policy Hackathon

End to End Data backed Proposal to achieve the aims of the Connecticut's Green Bank Storage Program, (in the first three years of the program at least 10% of residential installations must be in Low Income Households and 30% of installations must be in Underserved Communities.) .

OVERVIEW

As the winners of the MIT Policy Hackathon under the Environmental Justice Track, we devised a strategy using evidence-based methods for their equitable smart grid, storage mission.

 

Executive Summary:

Green Bank (GB) seeks to deploy battery power storage devices (580+MW) in housing units of Connecticut between 2022-2024. At least 40% installations have to be in low-income households and underserved, vulnerable communities. We approach this from a policy communication lens wherein the goal is community buy-in of incentives and high enrollment of people from target groups.

 

 We recommend a comprehensive outreach and behavioral nudge strategy to build trust and secure a buy-in among households already burdened with low income and/or other hardships. The three-pronged strategy comprises—

 

  1. Aligning GB and Utility Partners (Eversource + United Illuminating) on the messaging and visual language of intra- and inter-communication.  via a user-facing platform/app which also contains simplified financing procedures;
  2. Using behavioral nudges that help communities overcome status quo bias, reward enrolment and off-set loss aversion to the expense of securing the financing; and
  3.  Orienting outreach as an educational intervention rather than an advertising effort to include schools, co-ops, and civic bodies as allies.

 

THE LANDSCAPE

Connecticut Public Act 21-53 mandates the implementation of a power storage policy and pegs storage of 1000 MW by 2030 as the goal. However, the issue of power storage transcends legal compliance.

The people of Connecticut confront severe power outages and infrastructure failure owing to severe storms. For many low-income and underserved communities, it means loss of food and pumped water, for others who live on power-supported medical equipment it is a question of survival itself. While the “Take Back our Grid” Act holds utilities accountable by compensating customers with 250 USD for spoilt food and medicine, it doesn’t solve the problem of power outages and disruptions. Resilient power technologies, particularly energy storage devices can alleviate such troubles and are therefore necessary.

However, pre-existing hardships and debts among vulnerable communities can form perception barriers about new debt instruments. Besides other debts, unpaid electricity dues are a burden in themselves. With the Covid outbreak, this has been exacerbated.

 

When the question of affordability of such technologies arises, converging the efforts of green financing with financial inclusion strategies emerges as a viable option. (AFI and SOAS, 2020)

Here, drawing from Green Bank’s marketing approaches in previous financing services and creating locally specific outreach strategies are important to get low-income and underserved communities to enroll for the financing service at the offer.
