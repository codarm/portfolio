Parking Enforcement Optimization

Formulated & developed a traveling officer problem for Parking Enforcement to increase productivity, social justice & reduce predatory enforcement.  Used Genetic Algorithms, Ant Colony Optimization & Spatio-temporal clustering.`

OVERVIEW

Utilizing Urban Spaces with responsibility becomes very important to maintain the discipline and rhythm of the daily activities in a city. Parking spaces being urban dominant, it becomes important to enforce rules around their utility. Fair parking enforcement becomes critical for good transportation policy, helping in reducing congestion and dangerous conditions, also increasing turnover for the policymakers. As a result, proper management and optimization of parking enforcement officers’ time help in increasing access and making conditions safer for pedestrians and bicyclists.

The Solution for this wicked Problem of Parking & its Enforcement can be categorized into four steps as follows:

Performance Management: Measuring parking enforcement activity is important with many metrics & outcomes, and cities owe it to their constituencies to analyze the data. Issuing lots of citations doesn’t necessarily help improve the city experience. Hence the enforcement agencies should review the quality of citation issuance by every PEO.

Shift Improvement: Improving the cities performance can be achieved by optimizing the schedules, start-end time, and block navigation of the staff with the likelihood of finding citations using quantitative Spatio-temporal methods.  This could help the PEO ensure appropriate coverage, improve citation issuance and capture rates, and reduce downtime.

New Enforcement Zones: Enforcement boundaries aren’t modified for years, despite changing demand and use. Cities are often prone to dynamic contexts due to ever-evolving geospatial changes and are important to use this dynamic context data to improve the size and shape of the zones, resulting in reducing predatory enforcement, or the potential of PEO’s to focus too much on a neighborhood or even vehicles in some cases. Using algorithmic interventions to revise enforcement maps, nudging boundaries, and planning could help the PEO’s in achieving equity and increasing productivity by capturing dynamic trends.

Probability Mapping and Routing: Forecasting the likelihood of citations within the zones is just the first step of solutioning. These predictive algorithms do help the PEO understand where and when violations are most likely to occur in a zone but might not provide insights into how a PEO can best work an area to optimize time and productivity over a longer period of time. Developing an Optimized Solution for the traveling officer’s problem could help in the more efficient management of staff and their individual routing work schedules.

 

This section tries to tackle and answer the following questions

  1. Are enforcement zones being enforced equitably?
  2. Are safety violations being prioritized properly?
  3. Optimizing the number of PEOs distributed across various shift start and end times and the optimal route for capturing maximum priority centric violations
  4. Improving the correlation of enforcement hours to predicted citations
  5. Improving traffic flow by ensuring adequate enforcement during rush hour Increasing productivity

 

For the solutioning, I designed a Traveling Officer Problem which was customized to formulate a path-finding problem that aims to maximize the probability of capturing violations before the vehicles leave. One of the challenges which I faced was to extract effective features from the big Spatio-temporal data (Point of Interests, Parking Spots, Parking Violations, City Regions, etc)   and provide a data-driven solution to replace conventional solutions such as a simple rule-based manual system or single optimization methods. As a part of the latest efforts at Conduent Labs, I proposed and implemented an end-to-end learning and optimization framework that combines Auto-Encoder neural network and GCN’s for feature extraction, Spatio-temporal clustering of parking nodes, and path-finding methods to solve the Traveling Officer Problem.

 

This work led to a new framework to address the Traveling Officer Problem for parking enforcement as a Pilot. Unlike previous versions of the application deployed,  where the focus was on optimization methods and a simple exponential probability associated with leaving probability estimation, the new framework extracts spatiotemporal features from historical data and establish a more accurate probability model using clustering methods (like K-Means, DBSCAN, HDBSCAN & Dynamic Time Warping) Further we also applied optimization methods (like Generic algorithm, greedy and Ant colony optimization)  to larger dataset and combined with different leaving probability model. Based on the initial pilot studies with variable zone sizes and Block numbers, the results show that the precursor blocks of feature extraction & clustering could considerably improve the proposed framework output performance of the Optimization methods.

 

Here the traveling officer’s problem is an extension of the classic TSP algorithmic problem in the field of computer science and operations research. The problem is NP-complete as the number of combinations of traveling points grows larger. In the classic version of the problem, the salesman picks a city to start, travels through the remaining cities, and returns to the original city. In this version of parking enforcement, the problem slightly gets modified. The PEO starts at a specific zone/block, a fixed point on the grid. Then, moves to other blocks to capture the violations, which appear elsewhere on the grid. The PEO needs to visit the violation points in a time-sensitive scenario and also consider the priority of violation to obtain rewards. Rewards which are proportional to the time taken to do this (equivalent to the custom distance function, as each timestep moves one square on the grid).

With Dynamic sensors added to the puzzle, I have also been working on a reinforcement learning framework to aid and enhance the capture of parking violations. The main reasons  why I see RL offers some unique value for the parking enforcement problem are as follows:

  1. RL seems to perform well in high-dimensional spaces, when an approximate solution to a complex problem may be of greater value than an exact/optimal solution to a simpler problem.
  2. RL can do quite well in partially observed environments. When there are aspects of a problem we don’t know about and therefore can’t model, which is often the case in the real world (and we can pretend is the case with the parking violations & Dynamic city Contexts), RL’s ability to deal with the messiness is valuable.
  3. RL may have things to teach us! We’ve seen this to be the case with Go, and Dota 2, where the RL agent came up with innovative strategies that have later been adopted by human players. What if there are clever strategies we can use to solve versions of the Traveling officer’s Problem?
